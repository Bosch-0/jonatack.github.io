Reviewing is Voting

ON CHOOSING PRs TO REVIEW

Reviewing is not only the most effective way to contribute as a new contributor, it is also a powerful form of voting. Just as what we buy and do every day is the strongest way we can vote and cumulatively influence things, our choices here matter profoundly and cumulatively.

The Current State of Affairs

Several hundred pull requests are open at any given moment in Bitcoin Core waiting for review and testing, and the stack continues to grow higher -- 280 in November 2019, 350 at the end of January 2020!

Review is currently not keeping pace with PRs and issues (the latter are also up to nearly 800, from 700 a few months ago). Worse, the PRs that tend to get reviewed and merged quickly are the trivial, the easiest to review, and the newest. Valuable, high-priority PRs can be seen sitting for months or even years without enough reviewers, subjecting their authors to frustration, discouragement, and rebase hell until they abandon working on them.

The number of experienced people doing good, consistent review is surprisingly low. When I describe the situation to people, even to established VCs in the Bitcoin space, they tend to be astonished at how few people the system is depending on.

On any given day, you'll see the same 10-20 people reviewing on a daily basis. Of these, maybe half have the experience and available time/finances/predisposition for tackling review of the harder or larger PRs. That leaves roughly 10 consistent, experienced reviewers. The other half of the regular reviewers are reviewing the easier or more trivial PRs in their spare time.

Current Issues

This state of affairs creates several problems:

  1. High-priority, larger, or harder-to-review PRs can go for months (and even years) without sufficient review.
  2. PRs that provide important prevention but that are non-urgent, like adding missing test coverage, tend to sit for months without review or being merged.
  3. PR authors are incentivised to either leave the project out of frustration, or more often, open trivial and small PRs that have a chance of being reviewed and merged within the year (until the author tires of making this sort of contribution and aspires to greater things, before being discouraged again by the current state of affairs).
  4. We're running a continual risk of mal-investment -- of review resources going into minutae rather than catching a regression or vulnerablity, or other important priorities.

Reviewing is Voting

Given this state of affairs, clearly, which PRs we review with our limited time is an important choice, and one that might be underappreciated.

The PRs we review represent:

  • which areas we decide to contribute to
  • which project priorities matter to us
  • which authors we help
  • which kind of PRs we incentivise

Nowadays, I pay a considerable amount of attention to consciously choosing the right PRs to review. Here are my current criteria:

  • What: high-priority, higher-value, harder PRs over new/easy/trivial/annoying ones (knowing that they will see review anyway) unless they are urgent, interesting, or an area I know well
  • Who: PRs by people who also review PRs or test issues

sure, coding is more fun than reviewing. but reviewing is too vital here, and when i see trivial PRs i keep thinking, maybe too much, about the review resources they might be taking away from catching a regression or CVE

hopefully this review club can help encourage more reviewing but i'm thinking about how else we can encourage it, add incentives or social norms maybe, etc. these are good things to think about.

What To Do

Choose your reviews carefully! Don't squander time and social capital on the technical equivalent of junk food.

Continue to do more reviewing and testing than adding PRs to the backlog. Reviewing 5 to 15 PRs for every PR you open is a good range.

Happy contributing!
