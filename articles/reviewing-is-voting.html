Reviewing is Voting

ON CHOOSING PRs TO REVIEW

Reviewing is not only the most effective way to contribute as a new contributor, it is also a powerful form of voting. Just as what we buy and do every day is the strongest way we can vote and cumulatively influence things, our choices here matter profoundly and cumulatively.

The Current State of Affairs

Several hundred pull requests are open at any given moment in Bitcoin Core waiting for review and testing, and the stack continues to grow higher -- 280 in November 2019, 350 at the end of January 2020!

Review is currently not keeping pace with PRs and issues (the latter are also up to nearly 800, from 700 a few months ago).

Worse, the PRs that tend to get reviewed and merged quickly are the trivial, the easiest to review, and the newest. Valuable, high-priority PRs can be seen sitting for months or even years without enough reviewers, subjecting their authors to frustration, discouragement, and rebase hell until they abandon working on them.

The number of experienced people doing good, consistent review is surprisingly low. When I describe the situation to people, even to established VCs in the Bitcoin space, they tend to be astonished at how few people the system is depending on.

On any given day, you'll see the same 10-20 people reviewing on a daily basis. Of these, maybe half have the experience and available time/finances/predisposition for tackling review of the harder or larger PRs. That leaves roughly 10 consistent, experienced reviewers. The other half of the regular reviewers are reviewing the easier or more trivial PRs in their spare time.

Current Issues

This state of affairs creates several problems:

  1. High-priority, larger, or harder-to-review PRs can go for months (and even years) without sufficient review.
  2. Smaller PRs that provide important, non-urgent prevention, adding missing test coverage, tend to sit for months without review or being merged.
  3. Review and merges sometimes go to the squeaky wheel.
  4. Less-loud PR authors are incentivised to open more trivial and smaller PRs that have a better chance of being reviewed and merged within a reasonable time (until the author tires of making this sort of contribution and aspires to greater things, before being discouraged again by the current state of affairs). Any PR beyond a fairly small size or low level of complexity needs to be separated into bite-sized PRs that are easier to review.
  5. Things tend to be abandoned or never quite finished. Some contributors leave or take long periods of absence from the project out of frustration. Other shake it off but feel recurring discouragement.
  6. Of course, managing context, relevance, and building review alliances can make a huge difference. Bitcoin Core code reviews arguably represent a micro-economy of sorts. Getting your PRs reviewed without being annoying (preferably mainly by helping others and doing consistent review yourself) can be an art.
  7. Nevertheless, we're running a continual risk of mal-investment â€” scarce review resources squandered on the wrong things rather than catching a regression or vulnerability, fixing issues, improving test coverage, finishing up ongoing work that was has needed follow-ups, doing long-term work, and other priorities.
  8. Above all, not enough of the contributors do consistent review or follow the practice of 5-15 reviews or issues handled per PR opened.

Reviewing is Voting

Given this state of affairs, clearly, which PRs we review with our limited time is an important choice, and one that might be underappreciated.

The PRs we review represent:

  • what areas we contribute to
  • which project priorities matter to us
  • what kind of PRs we incentivise
  • ultimately, which behaviors we reward

Nowadays, I pay a considerable amount of attention to consciously choosing the right PRs to review. Here are my current criteria:

  • What: high-priority, higher-value, harder PRs over new/easy/trivial/annoying ones (knowing that they will see review anyway) unless they are urgent, interesting, or an area I know well.
  • Who: I want to reward people who do quality review or issue-testing, *particularly* if they are professional Bitcoin Core contributors, e.g. receiving a steady salary to work on Bitcoin open source.

Sure, coding is more fun than reviewing. but reviewing is too vital here, and when i see poorly-done PRs I keep thinking, maybe too much, about the review resources they might be taking away from catching a regression or CVE.

Hopefully the review club can help encourage more reviewing but i'm thinking about how else we can encourage it, add incentives or social norms maybe, etc. These are good things to think about.

What To Do

Continue to do more reviewing and testing than adding PRs to the backlog. Reviewing 5 to 15 PRs for every PR you open is a good range. Communicate that norm to people.

Choose PRs to review carefully. Don't squander time, diligence and care on the technical equivalent of junk food. Don't reward half-done PRs, and don't incentivise non-exceptional PRs added to the stack by authors who aren't giving back by reviewing consistently and commensurately.

Happy contributing!
